Interaction System

A great escape room needs to make the mundane, like searching through drawers and scanning through documents, feel both exciting and fun. Therefore, with the interaction system at the heart of Get Me Out's experience, I needed to focus on harvesting tactility and reactivity to the brink. Whilst snappy animation-based buttons and keypads were used, by and large I am most proud of the physics-based hold and grab mechanics.

Holding

Initial implementations of the hold system involved setting the held object as a child protruded in front of the first person camera. Whilst the approach worked, it felt rigid. Crucially, objects lacked momentum and wouldn't react to their surroundings. Thankfully, I have a trick that'll put a spring in any object's step — Oscillators!

As before, I used an empty child protruded in front of the first person camera, although this time to denote the target transform for held objects. I was then able to turn the hold behaviour on or off by simply enabling or disabling the oscillator component. From this simple approach, held objects gain physics-based easing and the ability to be displaced by the environment, whilst also conserving linear momentum — allowing for objects to be thrown at no extra cost. Adding additional features to the hold system, then comes naturally. For instance, the ability to control the distance at which an object is held is just a matter of controlling the target transform's local position.

All that's then missing is that objects won't rotate in response to collisions with the environment. This is remedied by doing all the same with a torsional oscillator component. Adding additional features once again comes naturally; the ability to rotate objects to inspect held objects at all angles is just a matter of rotating the target transform.

Grabbing

I distinguished grabbing from holding, as grabbed objects have unique limitations to their motion. For example, we grab desk drawers, which move restricted to a single fixed axis of motion. Additionally, we grab cupboard doors, which rotate along a single axis determined by their hinges, whilst remaining fixed in position. Due to the significant difference in these behaviours, I decided to subdivide grabbing into it's linear and torsional forms.

Whilst I was once again able to use oscillators for their many tactile and reactive gains, I soon encountered a new problem at hand. Namely, how could I accurately interpret how the player wants to move or rotate a grabbed object based on input? Favouring tactility over the mundane, I decided to try my hand at an approach based on look-direction.

Heads-Up Display

To further draw attention to interactable objects, I implemented a reactive reticle – this was the start of the Heads-Up Display (HUD). I later added the game timer to the HUD. Finally, I added interaction prompts to the HUD, which assisted new players in picking up the controls on the fly.
